If AI Can't Explain Itself, Should We Trust It?

Mahwish Farhan | Jun 27, 2026 min read

Artificial intelligence is now part of almost every area of life. It helps doctors read medical scans, helps banks detect fraud, and even helps governments make decisions about public services. But as AI becomes more powerful, one question becomes more important than ever:

Do we understand how AI makes its decisions?

This question leads us to one of the most important ethical issues in modern technology: transparency and explainability in AI systems.

This blog explores why transparency and explainability matter, how they connect to major AI ethics themes, and what real-world examples teach us.

The Black Box

Although AI offers many benefits, it also raises important ethical questions. One of the biggest concerns is that many AI systems work like a “black box.” They produce answers or decisions, but people often do not know how those decisions were made—even the engineers who built the system.

This lack of understanding creates problems when AI makes mistakes or unfair decisions.

The AI might be weighing hundreds of variables in ways no one fully understands. This matters because AI is increasingly making real decisions about real people. A system that cannot explain itself gives people no way to challenge a bad decision or detect when something is going wrong. And when errors happen—which they do—it becomes very hard to figure out what went wrong and how to fix it.

What Is Transparency in AI?

Transparency means being open about how an AI system works. It includes information about the data used to train the model, how decisions are made, and what the system is designed to do.

When we talk about transparency in AI, we mean two things:

  • Explainability – Can AI provide understandable reasons for its decisions in a way humans can understand?
  • Interpretability – Can experts look inside the system and understand how it actually works?

Transparency is the broader principle of being open about how an AI system works. Explainability is one part of transparency because it helps users understand why the AI reached a particular decision.

For example, imagine a bank uses AI to approve or reject loan applications.

Without explainability, the system simply says:

“Loan application rejected.”

With explainability, it might say:

“The application was rejected because the applicant’s debt-to-income ratio is above the acceptable limit and the credit history shows several missed payments.”

The second response allows people to understand the decision and, if necessary, challenge it.

At first glance, transparency and explainability may seem like technical concepts, but they are also ethical principles because they affect whether people are treated fairly and whether AI decisions can be questioned.

Why Transparency and Explainability Matter?

AI systems influence decisions that affect people’s lives. These decisions include:

  • Who gets hired
  • Who gets a loan
  • Which patient receives urgent care
  • How long someone stays in jail
  • What information people see online

When decisions have real consequences, opacity becomes an ethical problem.

Explainability helps answer questions such as:

  • Why was my job application rejected?
  • Why did the AI think this patient is low risk?
  • Why did the system deny my loan?

Without answers, people feel powerless.

With answers, they can challenge mistakes, correct unfairness, and trust the system.

The importance of transparency becomes even clearer when we look at real-world examples where AI systems have influenced people’s lives.

A Real-World Example: The COMPAS Algorithm

COMPAS (Correctional Offender Management Profiling for Alternative Sanctions) is a tool used in American courts to help judges decide matters such as bail and sentencing. It gives defendants a score based on how likely they are to reoffend.

Sounds helpful, but there was a big problem: nobody could see how the system reached its conclusions.

In 2016, journalists at ProPublica found that the system was treating people unfairly. Black defendants were almost twice as likely to be wrongly labelled as high risk compared to white defendants in similar situations.

However, there was one major problem: nobody could clearly understand how the algorithm reached its decision. The company that built COMPAS refused to share how it worked, calling it a trade secret.

In Wisconsin v. Loomis (2016), a defendant challenged this in court, arguing it was unfair to be sentenced based on a score that nobody could examine. The court still ruled against him.

That means someone could go to prison partly because of an algorithm that no one was allowed to question.

That is not just a technical flaw—it is an ethical failure.

Core Principles of AI Ethics

The COMPAS case demonstrates why transparency is closely connected to several core principles of AI ethics.

1. Fairness

The COMPAS algorithm failed the fairness principle because it produced higher risk scores for Black defendants compared to white defendants with similar records. A fair system should treat everyone equally, regardless of race or background.

2. Accountability

Accountability requires that someone is responsible for the decisions an AI system influences. With COMPAS, judges relied on the scores, but the company refused to reveal how the algorithm worked. Without transparency, accountability becomes almost impossible.

3. Transparency

Transparency means people should be able to understand how decisions are made. COMPAS was a black box, and defendants could not challenge or question their risk scores because the company kept the model secret.

4. Non-Maleficence (Do No Harm)

The algorithm caused real harm by labelling some defendants as high risk when they were not. This led to longer sentences, fewer opportunities for parole, and lasting personal consequences.

5. Respect for Human Dignity

Every person deserves to be treated with dignity and respect. When an AI system assigns a risk score without explanation and influences a person’s freedom, it reduces them to a number. COMPAS did not allow defendants to understand or challenge the score, undermining their dignity and right to a fair process.

Transparency and AI Governance

Because of cases like COMPAS and recognizing these risks, governments and international organizations have introduced AI governance rules that promote transparency and accountability and guide how AI systems are developed and used.

The European Union’s AI Act, which came into force in 2024, is one of the strongest examples. It requires that AI systems used in sensitive areas—such as hiring, education, credit decisions, and law enforcement—must be transparent and explainable. People affected by these systems have the right to know how a decision was made.

Similarly, the OECD AI Principles, adopted by more than 40 countries, identify transparency and explainability as core requirements for responsible AI development.

These frameworks show a growing international consensus:

Explainability is not optional—it is a basic requirement.

How Ethical Principles Support Transparent and Explainable AI?

Transparency and explainability are essential for building ethical AI systems.

Ethical AI frameworks encourage developers to create AI that is fair, accountable, and transparent so users can understand how decisions are made.

AI governance supports these principles by establishing policies and oversight to ensure AI systems are monitored, tested for bias, and used responsibly.

Soft ethics goes beyond legal requirements by encouraging organizations to be open about how AI works and how data is used, even when the law does not require it.

At the same time, applying data science ethics to AI ensures that data is collected and used fairly, privacy is respected, and biased outcomes are reduced.

Together, these principles help make AI systems more transparent, explainable, and trustworthy for everyone.

Conclusion: A Future Where AI Speaks Clearly

The COMPAS story is a powerful reminder that AI transparency is not just a nice feature—it is an ethical necessity.

When AI systems make consequential decisions without explanation, bias goes unchecked, people lose their ability to defend themselves, and trust breaks down.

The good news is that we are not powerless.

AI governance frameworks like the EU AI Act are setting new standards. Researchers are developing explainable AI tools. And a growing number of organizations are embracing the spirit of soft ethics, doing more than the law requires because they genuinely care about getting it right.

But in the end, it comes down to a simple principle:

If an AI system is making decisions that affect real people, it should be able to explain itself.

That is not too much to ask. In fact, it is the very least we should expect.

If AI cannot explain its decisions, it becomes difficult to trust its judgments.

Transparency is therefore not just a technical feature; it is the foundation of ethical and trustworthy AI.
