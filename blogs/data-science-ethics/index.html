The Illusion of Anonymity: When Data Science Ethics Gets Lost in the Survey

Mahwish Farhan | Jun 28, 2026 min read

Ethics in Data Collection and Analysis

In data science, it’s easy to focus on numbers and results, but behind every dataset are real people who trust us with their information. How that information is collected and used can have important ethical implications.

Most of us have completed a survey after finishing a course, training session, or workshop. These surveys are often presented as a way for organizations to gather feedback for improvements. In this scenario, participants are asked to complete a ten-question survey after finishing a course. To access the survey, they must log in using their organization username and password. Although the survey does not ask for their name, it states that all responses will remain anonymous. Executive management plans to use the survey results to identify courses that need improvement, and data analysts are responsible for analyzing the feedback and providing recommendations.

While this survey may seem straightforward, it raises several ethical concerns related to the collection of data and the responsible use of it.

Ethical Issues in the Survey Design and Sample Question

1. False Anonymous Claim

The first ethical concern that stands out is anonymity. The survey claims that responses are anonymous, but participants are required to log in with their organizational account. Even if the survey itself does not ask for a person’s name, the system still records login credentials. Because of this, participants may not truly be anonymous.

This leads to another important ethical concept: transparency. Participants are not given a clear explanation of how privacy is protected. Organizations have a responsibility to be clear about how data is collected and used. If participants believe their responses cannot be linked to them, but the system makes that possible, the survey is not fully transparent. People should know exactly what information is being collected before they agree to participate.

2. Lack of Informed Consent

The issue of informed consent is also relevant. Informed consent means that individuals understand:

  • What data is being collected
  • How it will be used
  • Who will see it
  • Whether it can be linked back to them

The survey description does not explain any of this. Simply stating “anonymous” is not enough. Without clear information, participants cannot make an informed decision about whether to participate.

3. Survey Question Design

The sample survey question raises concerns as well. At first glance, it seems reasonable. However, it combines all course objectives into a single rating. A participant may feel that some objectives were achieved while others were not. By asking about all objectives together, the survey may not capture an accurate picture of the course’s strengths and weaknesses.

Additionally, not every participant may clearly remember all aspects of the course. This could affect the quality and reliability of the data collected.

Other Ethical Issues That Could Be Present

1. Data Privacy and Security

One major issue is where the data is stored, whether it is encrypted, and who has access to it. Without clear policies, there is a risk of violating data privacy and security. Survey responses may contain opinions, criticisms, or comments that participants expect to remain confidential. If the organization does not properly secure this information, unauthorized individuals could gain access. Protecting collected data is an important ethical responsibility.

2. Limited Time Window

The survey is available for a short time. This may systematically exclude groups. Participants who are unavailable during that time—busy, traveling, or managing personal responsibilities—may not get the chance to respond, making the results unbalanced and unfair.

3. Biased Response

Another issue is bias. If participants alter their responses because they fear being identified, the data will not accurately reflect their true opinions. This is known as response bias. As a result, management may make decisions based on misleading information rather than genuine feedback.

4. Misuse of Data

Another possible concern is the future use of the data. The survey is intended to improve courses, but what if management uses the survey results to evaluate instructors or departments? This could cause harm. Surveys intended for improvement should not become tools for scrutiny.

Recommendations for Executive Management

1. Remove Login Requirement

If the survey is anonymous, the system must not require personal credentials. One option would be to separate the login from the survey. Other alternatives include:

  • Anonymous tokens
  • Open access links
  • Temporary survey IDs

2. Provide Clear Informed Consent

The organization should clearly explain how the survey works. Participants should know what information is collected, whether login data is stored, who has access to the responses, and how the results will be used. Greater transparency helps build trust and supports informed consent.

3. Improve Question Design

Survey questions should be clear, neutral, and free from absolute language. Instead of asking participants whether the course met all objectives in a single question, each objective could be evaluated separately. This would provide more detailed and actionable feedback for course improvement.

4. Establish Ethical Governance

The organization should establish strong data governance practices. Access to survey data should be limited to authorized personnel, and the information should be stored securely. Clear policies should define how long the data will be retained and how it may be used in the future.

What If Management Rejects the Recommendations?

As a data analyst, ethical responsibilities do not disappear simply because management disagrees with the concerns raised.

1. Document My Concerns

I would create and maintain a written record of identified risks and recommendations. This demonstrates professional accountability and ensures that concerns have been properly communicated. It also protects me and shows due diligence.

2. Limit My Analysis to Ethical Boundaries

If required to deliver analysis, I would include clear warnings and limitations. I would explain that the findings are based on a survey that may not be truly anonymous and had a limited participation window. This ensures decision-makers understand that the conclusions should be treated as directional, not definitive.

3. Encourage Ethical Data Use

I would recommend using survey results strictly for course improvement and avoiding disciplinary interpretations. This approach reflects the principle of beneficence, which emphasizes doing good while preventing potential harm.

I would not simply go along with the process and pretend there are no problems. If I believe there are concerns about privacy, anonymity, or data quality, I should be honest about them. As a data analyst, it is important to act responsibly, respect people’s information, and be transparent about any limitations in the data.

Conclusion

This survey shows how easily ethical issues can hide beneath a simple form. What looks straightforward actually raises concerns about false anonymity, pressured participation, unclear questions, and sampling bias.

This scenario highlights five key ethical concepts: informed consent, data privacy, transparency, sampling bias, and accountability.

As data professionals, we are not just analyzing numbers; we are responsible for handling information that belongs to real people. When a system fails ethically, it’s our job to point out the issues, advocate for better practices, and refuse to contribute to harm—even when leadership disagrees. The survey can be fixed, but if leadership ignores the issues, the analyst must make the ethical concerns impossible to ignore.
